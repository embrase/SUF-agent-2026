## Reinventing the startup ecosystem

For over a decade, the Startupfest value proposition has been simple: Come to the event, learn, network, and find funding or companies to fund. But the rise of agentic generative AI is upending most of the economics of the startup world, and we need to respond to it. *AI presents an existential threat to Startupfest's current business model*, and we are adapting accordingly.

If you consider the life of a startup, it has three key phases:
1. *Pre-revenue investment*: It builds the product, which incurs costs. On-demand tools like cloud computing reduce the up-front capital expense and initial investment needed (in the form of a loan, venture capital, founder funding, or some other financial instrument.) The startup needs resources well before it generates revenue. The exception is kickstarter and patrongage models, which are a valid approach to de-risking and funding a project for some startups. These costs represent how much the company is losing in the pre-revenue phase (the  "burn" of the company).
2. *It launches the product*, at which point revenues begin to flow into the organization. If it charges up front for the product (i.e. a one-time software license; a physical book) then revenues come in relatively quickly. If it charges for its services in a different way (for example, a monthly subscription, or a percentage fee of each transaction) then the revenues will be slower to accumulate (for example, on a per-user-per-month basis, or with transaction volume).
3. *Profitability.* At some point, if the company is thriving, monthly revenues will exceed burn.The company may be able to pay back the up-front investment (for example, paying down a loan), or pay its founders more (if it's a lifestyle business) or buy out investors for a multiple of their investment. In many cases, however, the company may be a fast-growing business in a potentially lucrative sector. When this happens it will often take on additional investment to scale, and burn may continue to exceed revenues.

We can trace the pattern of costs and revenues with two lines. In the pre-revenue phase, costs climb steeply but revenues are zero. At the moment of product launch, revenues begin to climb steeply (if the company charges up front) or gradually (for subscription or transaction models). At profitability the revenue line crosses the cost line.
There's a problem with this. The traditional VC/startup model relies on *the area under the curve*. This is a function of how many costs the startup will incur (its total burn) before hitting profitability. The longer it takes to build a product, and the higher the costs to build it, the more investment the company will need. But AI reduces both *the cost ot building a product* and *the time it takes to build it*. This squashes the area under the curve: Many mid-market VCs, accelerators, and ecosystem players will be eliminated.
- Mid-sized VCs don't have the money to invest in big, deep-tech deals; and don't have the capacity to manage a very large number of small investments.
- Accelerators typically offer a "demo day" to VCs, but with an AI helping the founder there's much less value for the accelerator to offer.
- Ecosystem players (such as lawyers, accountants, grant application assistance programs, etc.) are white-collar services that are being quickly eliminated by AI tools. Cash-strapped founders won't need them.
At the same time, the reduced barriers to entry in a market mean more niche players who target a specific segment, instead of a few very large players. Instead of Uber, think cars for people in wheelchairs, people partying, people who need to do their groceries; instead of a CRM, think a CRM for yoga studios, or for dentists, or for tattoo parlors. This means the total addressable market for any given startup is smaller, and we see logistic (capped) growth rather than something purely exponential. This was what Alistair Croll covered in his *million tiny horses* talk in 2025, and it will only get more pronounced.

To survive, Startupfest cannot rely on its past business model and partners. It must recognize that tomorrow's startups will look fundamentally different: Smaller, faster, and equipped with an "AI co-founder", a collection of agents and skills that carry out many of the tasks humans would, from technical to marketing to administrative functions.

It must also identify the major competitive moats that remain, or are created, in an AI shift.

## What will still be true

That doesn't mean startups have nothing to learn from startupfest. Quite the contrary; there are new competitive moats and differentiators that matter more. In a talk at Startupfest 2025, conference chair Alistair Croll gave a talk entitled *A million tiny horses* that reflected on this.

### From Client-server to SaaS
- The first wave of software startups used the client-server model. Users bought software on a floppy disk and installed it. Siebel Systems was the darling of this era.
- When Web 2.0 came along, letting developers build interactive websites, we began to deliver software via the web browser rather than having it run natively on the desktop.

A SaaS company had many competitive advantages over client-server predecessors:
- They can bypass enterprise IT departments and "land and expand" its sales, because there was no installation process that IT could control--just a URL to click
- They can sell to a single user for a small amount per month. This meant Salesforce could charge $50 per seat, far less than the $5M Siebel expected a customer to have before they'd consider selling their product.
- They can use a "freemium" strategy to give prospects a taste of the product, then charge a monthly fee once the customer hit a particular threshold.
- They can deploy far more quickly, because the software was already running on cloud-based servers. Siebel had a multi-month installation process the customer needed to pay for; Salesforce just needed a web connection.
- They're selling much more than just software; they're selling operations. Companies who previously couldn't use enterprise software because they couldn't afford to operate it could now use that functionality in the cloud, which dramatically expanded the total addressable market for software.
- Customers could pay for their CRM with savings in IT, since they no longer needed to deploy and maintain hardware and networks.
- Every prospect effectively installed the top of their own funnel, because they could start using the app for free and then naturally be led towards a paid tier when they hit a milestone in data, users, or functionality.
- The SaaS company could learn how users were using the app, and improve it based on those usage patterns rather than customer anecdotes collected only occasionally.
- The SaaS provider could test features on a segment of users before rolling them out.
- Demos were trivially easy, shortening the sales cycle.
- The SaaS provider could offer integrations with other SaaS platforms, adding functionality (i.e. tying payments to Stripe or Paypal; launching documents in Google Drive, etc.)
- Rolling out fixes was easy, because the code was running on servers, not on the customer's IT. Once the customer approved the SaaS company it approved the fixes.
- Adding adjacent product offerings (as Hubspot did with marketing automation) was easy because customer accounts and data existed already, which made a "land and expand" sales strategy much easier.
- The SaaS provider could deploy redundant systems and amortize the cost of doing so across many customers, something no individual customer could afford, thereby delivering better performance and resiliency than IT-run systems.
- The SaaS company enjoyed network effects: The more people using the platform, the more it could show comparative data (how was a company doing against its peers?) and offer connections between users (Freshbooks lets you invoice and pay within its system if both parties are using Freshbooks.)
- Copy protection became less of an issue (because the software vendor was no longer installing software by disks, where the license was tied to the installation, but instead tied to the user's account.) The SaaS provider didn't care if you ran their app in five places as long as they were all the same customer--in fact, they preferred it.
- The app could link to contextual documentation based on what the user was doing, updated constantly by the customers.
- If a customer found a bug, they could report it with context (since the app was logging data somewhere the vendor could see it) and then roll out a fix to all users.
- Since the entire system lived online, the SaaS provider enjoyed viral growth paths. Hotmail attached a short message to every email it sent, which led to rapid adoption as every message became a promotion.
- The SaaS provider piggybacked on browser enhancements. As browsers gained new features like autocomplete, tab navigation, Javascript, etc., the SaaS company benefitted from them (whereas for a desktop client, they had to build these features themselves.)
- The separation of app from data meant the SaaS company could create multiple front-ends for a user, based on their role, device, or the task they were performing. This was possible in the client-server world but meant much more work, but since browsers were cross-device and ubiquitous, it was easy to maintain variants of one front-end codebase.

These are just a few of the differences between SaaS and client-server software that have nothing to do with functionality. There are many more. And companies that changed their business model from client-server to SaaS survived, while those that didn't perished.

Nothing embodies this better than Siebel Systems. The company was once the fastest-growing firm in the world, delivering a 782,978% CAGR in five years. When it became obvious that the web was popular, it built a web front-end. It changed its user interface, but not its business model. As a result, it was eventually sold for parts at a fraction of its one-time peak value.

By contrast, Concur Technologies, which also started as a client-server company, changed its business model to SaaS completely and end-of-lifed its client-server offering after the crash of the tech sector in 2001. It grew so fast that it was sold to SAP in 2014 for $8.3B in what was then the largest-ever SaaS acquisition.

### From SaaS to AI

A similar transition is happening now. Companies that continue to act like SaaS providers in an AI age will go the way of Siebel; those that make the shift will be Concur. So the question is: what are the new moats for a startup in an AI age?

One way to think about this is that we're entering the "age of aaS": When the software functionality is removed from SaaS, what's left?

- The value isn't functionality, it's certainty of delivered outcomes. Customers don't want "a list of prospects,", they want "confirmed meetings." They don't want "my expenses are scanned," they want "my taxes are filed and compliant." They don't want "my invoices are sent," they want "my bank balance is going up predictably."
- Distribution matters more. If a company has customer reach, it can aggressively move customers to new offerings *provided it is willing to cannibalize past revenue*. It needs to walk away from the seductive past towards a realistic, sustainable future. This is akin to Blockbuster being willing to give up customer late fees (which funded the business, but punished customers.) Loom and Otter.ai took tens of millions of investment for functionality that can be replicated by an agentic coding AI in a couple of days

Some other lenses to think abou this:
#### Jevons' Paradox: What New Demand Emerges When Knowledge Work Is Nearly Free?

When coal engines got more efficient, coal consumption *increased* — new uses emerged that weren't worth pursuing when coal was expensive. The standard Jevons take on AI is "more software." That's too obvious. Here's what's actually coming:

**Adversarial verification becomes the default.** Right now, nobody hires a second team to independently rebuild their product and compare results — it would cost 2x. When building is nearly free, you can afford to build everything twice: once to ship, once to attack. Red-teaming at the unit level. Every product ships with its own adversary. Every strategy document gets an automated devil's advocate. This is a new category of demand that nobody is budgeting for, and it will consume as much compute as the original production.

**Speculative prototyping replaces proposals.** Instead of writing decks and RFPs, you build the thing. "Here's what your product would look like if you hired us" — delivered as a working prototype, not a slide. The entire sales motion for consulting, agencies, and enterprise software shifts from persuasion to demonstration. The RFP process, a multi-billion-dollar industry of paperwork, dies — replaced by competitive build-offs.

**Compliance becomes code.** Regulation currently exists as text that lawyers interpret. When AI can translate regulation into executable test suites, companies will run continuous compliance checks the way they run continuous integration. Expect regulatory bodies themselves to start shipping machine-readable rules, creating a new interface between government and industry that requires its own infrastructure.

**Litigation simulation.** If you can model a lawsuit before filing — generate the opposing arguments, simulate discovery, estimate outcomes — the economics of legal disputes change. More lawsuits get *evaluated* (because evaluation is cheap), but paradoxically more get *settled* (because both sides can see the likely outcome). The demand is for "pre-litigation modeling" — a service category that barely exists today.

**Continuous organizational self-diagnosis.** Currently, companies hire consultants for periodic strategy reviews. When analysis is nearly free, you can run the equivalent of a McKinsey engagement continuously — monitoring strategy-market fit, organizational health, competitive positioning — as a background process. Not "more consulting" but "always-on strategic awareness."

#### Herbert Simon's Scarcity: What Does AI Consume That Becomes Scarce?

Simon's insight: when something becomes abundant, whatever it consumes becomes the bottleneck. Information abundance made attention scarce. What does AI abundance consume?

**Shared context.** When everyone's output triples but nobody has time to read each other's work, organizations lose coherence. The thing that degrades isn't individual productivity — it's *mutual awareness*. Meetings used to be where context synchronized. In an AI-saturated workplace, produced work overwhelms every synchronization mechanism we have. Teams of highly productive individuals with no idea what the other teams are doing. The more AI produces, the less anyone knows what anyone else produced. Shared context becomes the bottleneck for every organization above about 20 people.

**The patience for depth.** When you can get a 70% solution instantly, the willingness to invest in reaching 95% evaporates. This isn't about individual skill atrophy. It's about *organizational tolerance for mastery*. Why spend six months developing deep domain expertise when AI gives you a functional approximation in minutes? You end up with a world full of 70% solutions and nobody who can tell you why they're wrong. The 70% looks good enough until it encounters the 30% it doesn't understand, and then it fails in ways nobody can diagnose.

**Reconstructable decision history.** When a human does work, there's a trail — emails, drafts, meetings, revisions. When an agent does work, there's a prompt and an output. The intermediate reasoning is opaque or ephemeral. The more AI does, the less anyone can reconstruct *why* things happened the way they did. This matters for auditing, for learning from failure, for institutional memory, for litigation.

**The ability to converge.** When producing a plan takes weeks, the slowness itself forces alignment — people negotiate during the process. When AI generates five competing plans in an hour, production is cheap but *agreement* isn't cheaper. It's actually harder, because now there are more options to argue about and less shared suffering invested in any one of them. Decision-making speed hasn't improved; only option-generation speed has. Every additional option AI produces makes convergence harder, not easier.

#### Edward Tenner's Revenge Effects: How AI Bites Back

Tenner's core insight: technologies designed to solve problems often create new problems of the same severity, just in a different place. The revenge effects of AI are already emerging — and they have data behind them.

**The maintenance inversion.** Every piece of vibe-coded software has zero institutional knowledge behind it. Nobody understands the architecture because nobody designed it in the human-legible sense. When it breaks, you can't debug it by reasoning about the design, because there was no deliberate design process. You can ask AI to fix it, but that's a new prompt in a new context — the original reasoning is lost.

A 2024 fintech case study made the cost concrete: in Q1, the team shipped 47% more features, commits up 61%, PRs nearly doubled. By Q3, the product was bleeding users — crashes under moderate load, three critical vulnerabilities, technical debt that would take 18 months to stabilize. The 2024 DORA report found that a 25% increase in AI tool adoption correlated with a 7.2% decrease in delivery stability and a 1.5% decrease in throughput. Duplicate code blocks went up 8-fold. Copy-pasted code increased 48%. Code churn doubled. By year two, unmanaged AI code drives maintenance costs to 4x traditional levels. *Fixing things costs more than building them.* This inverts every assumption in software economics.

**The coordination explosion.** The work intensification described in Act 2 has a mathematical consequence most organizations miss. Three simultaneous projects have 3 potential conflicts. Ten have 45. Thirty (easy when starting costs nothing) have 435. Coordination overhead grows quadratically while project-spawning grows linearly. At some point coordination cost exceeds production cost — but there's no natural signal telling you when you've crossed that line. Without shared task management across human and machine collaborators, you get task sprawl — an ever-expanding surface area of half-started, partially-monitored work. The need isn't for more powerful AI but for better ways to track what's happening.

**Expertise illegibility.** When AI makes everyone's output look competent, you lose the ability to distinguish between someone who genuinely understands a domain and someone who has a good prompt.

The legal profession has already made this painfully visible. In *Noland v. Land of the Free*, nearly every quoted authority in the plaintiff's briefs was fabricated — entire passages falsely attributed to published opinions, cited cases that didn't exist. In California, two law firms were fined $31,000 for submitting a brief with fake citations generated by Google Gemini and Westlaw Precision. In Colorado, a federal judge identified nearly thirty defective citations. Researchers have compiled a list of at least 155 court decisions worldwide involving hallucinated content. The briefs looked properly formatted and legally plausible. The errors were only discovered upon citation verification — not through surface inspection.

This is Tenner's revenge at its purest: the tool that makes everyone look skilled makes skill invisible. Hiring, promotion, vendor selection, expert testimony — every trust relationship that depends on assessing competence is degraded.

**The accountability vacuum.** When a human makes a decision, there's a decision-maker. When an AI agent makes 400 micro-decisions completing a task, who's accountable for the bad ones? Not the prompter — they didn't anticipate specific decisions. Not the AI company — they disclaim outputs. Not the AI — it has no legal standing. You've created a system that makes more decisions faster while making each decision harder to attribute. This isn't a governance problem solvable with better policies. It's a structural incompatibility between how AI works and how accountability works.

**The homogenization trap.** AI is trained on the aggregate of human output, which means it converges toward consensus perspectives. When everyone uses AI to think, write, and decide, there's a subtle gravitational pull toward the center.

Research on 2,200 college admissions essays found that human essays consistently show higher creativity than GPT-4 at both individual and collective levels — and the diversity gap widens with scale. The more AI essays in a pool, the more homogeneous the aggregate. A study of restaurants in Milan during a ChatGPT-free period found a 15% decrease in lexical similarity and 12% decrease in syntactic similarity — meaning that with AI access, everyone's copy was converging on the same patterns.

The revenge effect: the tool designed to augment human thinking narrows the variance of thought. Homogeneous thinking is fragile thinking. It produces organizations that all make the same mistakes at the same time, markets that all move in the same direction, and strategies that all look alike.

## AI, inside and out

This change isn't just about what companies and business models survive; it's also about how those companies operate. AI is changing the value customers will pay for, but also how that value is created. A company cannot embrace AI in its product offerings without also embracing it internally, which means different systems, cost structures, expenses, organizational charts, and investments:

- Changes to capital efficiency: Startups can use AI to prototype features, replacing the traditional "get out of the building" phase with "try the product."
- Less dillution because startups don't need to raise as much money to get to market, since they have pay-as-you-go developer tools.

There are also some important fundamental lessons for everyone to understand regarding AI:
- Deterministic and nondeterministic computing: AI makes mistakes. In pursuit of goals it can be creatively reckless. Security issues, loss of data, and an inability to troubleshoot complex systems without more AI all matter. CI/CD pipelines will change to verify each step of deployments; we'll see AIs writing code to perform repetitive tasks.
- The red queen effect: Everyone will use what's working for others, meaning adoption takes off whether you like it or not. We're already seeing "AI Veganism" as a reaction to this, but most for-profit organizations will have no choice but to become agentic.
- The VC Schism of 2026: VC firms are splitting into "seedstrapping" small investors whose portfolios aren't underwater; and big investors like Lightspeed and Andreesen Horowitz who can restructure themselves as investment banks, no longer required to run a series of funds or liquidate companies when they go public. These big firms are buying up hospital chains and then pouring their AI startups into those companies because they believe that the resulting revenues will exceed the proceeds of IPOs for startups.
- Using someone else's app will be like chewing someone else's gum. Custom, ephemeral interfaces become the norm.
- Companies need to retool their websites for agents. An agent is a first-class citizen on the web these days, and forcing it to click through ads or navigate popups is problematic, error-prone, and inefficient. For many businesses, your first and only client is a chatbot acting on behalf of a user.

# THE BIG IDEA

Beyond this cosmetic theme, Startupfest 2026 is about the rupture that the startup ecosystem is undergoing thanks to agentic AI. All of the economics of software itself have changed. This is not unprecedented:

- First, a computer was a literal set of wires. You programmed it by wiring it.
- Then, the computer became *programmable* with little switches that were flipped by hand.
- Then, the interface changed: Punch cards separated the act of writing the code from the act of configuring the machine to run it. Terminals and timesharing sliced the computer into individual user environments, which was the start of virtualization.
- Then, we got a compiler. The human could work with easier, higher-level languages (COBOL, FORTRAN, BASIC, and so on) that the computer translated into machine code.
- Then, the software split into the operating system and the apps. The operating system offered complete functions (keyboard, clipboard, graphics, printer drivers) and abstracted the hardware away from the apps. This made it possible for apps to be written for a wide variety of hardware.
- Version control, code repositories, forking branches, and pull requests allowed developers to collaborate on code simultaneously.
- Cloud computing separated the physical hardware from the computer. This allowed scaling, elasticity, and portability.
- The web gave us a different kind of separation, allowing many people to use an app with a standard browser; initially, the browser was a "dumb terminal" but JavaScript and WebAssembly made it a place where code could run locally.

Notice that each of these split the idea of a "computer" -- a system that turned inputs into outputs. And with each split, two things happened:
1. Whatever was below the split became a utility. Instead of every company having a relatively small IT department and a server room with a hundred machines, a few big hyperscalers have large IT departments and data centers around the world with millions of machines. They competed on cost, reliability, and economies of scale. And these utilities tried to "eat" the layer above them: In early operating systems, search and fonts were applications you purchased; now, they're built into the operating system. There's even a name for this: *sherlocking*, which refers to when Apple added search to iOS and put Sherlock, a desktop search app, out of business.
2. Whatever was above the split got democratized. When the terminal, or human-readable code, or web browsers launched, an order of magnitude more people were able to build software. These compete on taste, reputation, network effects like having many users, exclusive access to proprietary data, and so on. Consider blogging: Early on, it was nerds who knew HTML; then it was Wordpress; now it's just Facebook posts.

At the top of all this was still "apps." If you were building an app, you had a number of functions:
- The market need: why and for whom am I making this?
- The Marketing Requirements Document (MRD): What is my rationale and product roadmap?
- The Product Requirements Document (PRD): What is my product?
- The Technical Requirements Document (TRD): How will I build my product?
- The design: What will my product look like?
- The code: How will I build my product?
- The testing: How will I test my product?
- The deployment: How will I deploy my product?
- The maintenance: How will I maintain my product?

To build these things, a software organization created an engineering environment. Some things were personal (EMACS or VIM, choice of terminal) and others were standardized (GitHub repositories, documentation rules, CI/CD pipelines that automated the shipping and testing of code, etc.) Writing the actual code is a very small part of a software developer did in 2022.

It's that "building an app" part that AI is now changing. AI is another split in the "computer monolith". You now have the human (providing impetus, deciding on the goals, setting the tone, absorbing the liability, etc.) and below it an environment (chatbot, Coworker, CLI; plus skills, memories, and context.) The fields of *harness configuration* and *context engineering* become more important. In many ways, they're the new software engineering.

We're currently seeing an explosion of harnesses. Gastown, OpenClaw and other Claw-based frameworks; new shells designed to manage many concurrent agents; IDEs like Cursor or Antigravity; skills like Superpowers that give an AI instructions on how to think through a problem; MCPs that tell an AI how to control other systems or talk to other machines; and so on. These will eventually resolve into best practices and a few dominant frameworks, just like the early days of the web. And there will be  bespoke harnesses for things like military use, high-frequency trading, and other areas based on constraints. If your app has to be fast, or written in a certain language, or run on a certain platform, you may need to constrain it.

But AIs aren't just software tools. They're translation engines. An AI can translate anything. Not just English to French: from customer requirements to a specification; from plain language instructions to working code; from a PDF to a presentation; from a spreadsheet to a dashboard; from a conversation to a set of actions; from a set of actions to a set of instructions; from a financial statement to the terms of a loan; from law to policy to regulation to training materials; and so on. Give an AI a rulebook and it will tell you how best to play a game.

They do this by understanding the underlying structure of a thing. The French word for lighthouse is "phare". A simple translation from French to English would just have a lookup. There's no magic in looking something up. But if you give an AI enough examples (during its training) then it extracts a generalized *meaning* of lighthouse. It associates it with concepts like "tower" and "light" and "storm" and "shipwreck." This generalization is called Grokking; we can literally see it happen: given enough examples, the AI suddenly gets really good at inferring things it's never seen before.

There are some serious philosophical questions here: is language inseparable from understanding? Are there deeper underlying patterns in math, and gameplay, and software development that humans can't perceive, but that are revealed in the weights of an AI? But we'll leave those for philosophers for now and look at the practical consequences of a universal translator that can extract deep structure from whatever it's trained on, and turn that into useful outputs.

With this framing, an AI is more than a coding tool. Tey Bannerman offers four ways to actually interact with AI tools based on the *outcome you want*.

- **Compression** — You're overwhelmed and need clarity.
- **Expansion** — You're stuck and need options.
- **Reflection** — You need to stress-test your own reasoning.
- **Execution** — The thinking is done and you need it produced.

The key here is *what you need* not *what you can do*.

**For a modern startup**, this means that you have a variety of agents performing translation work and extracting the underlying structure of your business, and helping you not only with execution, but also reflection, expansion, and clarity.

We're simplifying this idea into the notion of an **agentic co-founder**. It's an imperfect name, but it captures the idea that you now have a partner in your business who can help you with all aspects of it. And frankly, if you don't have a partner like this, you're probably already out of business and don't know it yet.

Other players in the startup ecosystem also have agentic sidekicks. Agents can help with marketing, due diligence, financial modeling, competitive analysis, curriculum creation, and more. Bluntly: Everyone coming to Startupfest should have an agentic co-founder

## BRINGING YOUR AGENTIC CO-FOUNDER TO STARTUPFEST

So what does it mean to bring your agent to a conference? Plenty of people have used AIs to improve their conference experience: Asking it what talks you should attend, or which restaurants are nearby for meetings, or sending invitations to others who are going. But we want to take things further. We want your AI agent to be an equal, an attendee who is coming to the conference with you.

So we started thinking about what a truly agentic conference would look like.

### AI CALL FOR PROPOSALS

Conferences often have content: talks, panels, and so on. These are often suggested through a Call for Proposals / Call For Papers / Call for Speakers (generally known as a CFP.) The proposals are submitted, then ranked and reviewed (either by the organizers, a committee, or in some cases like SXSW by attendees.) The best proposals are accepted, the speakers work on their slides, and then they take the stage to deliver a talk.

What would it be like for your Agentic Co-Founder to propose a talk? To vote on the best proposals? To deliver a talk?

### AI TRADESHOW

Many conferences have a vendor expo where companies can showcase their products or services. Their goals are to find customers, meet potential partners, recruit employees, secure financing, and otherwise help their business grow.

What would it be like for your Agentic Co-Founder to set up and host a trade show booth? To attend a tradeshow hosted by other AIs, and set up meetings between their human counterparts?

### AI PANEL DISCUSSION

Similar to a Call for Proposals, we could have debates between two AI co-founders and a moderator. The AIs would need to be trained on the topic and given context to remain on topic, but it could be entertaining.

### AI CONFERENCE DEBRIEF

Many employees write up their conference experience to share what they did, who they met, and what they learned with co-workers. What would we need to make available to an AI (transcripts, images, etc.) to do the same?

### THE startupfest.md FILE

Most conferences have a Code of Conduct that tells humans how to behave. It generally focuses on *misbehavior*, since humans have had a lifetime of experience with social norms. AI doesn't have this. An AI code of conduct would be more like a set of skills, goals, and instructions that define:

- **What to do before the event to prepare for it**: Prepare a bio and compaby brief; propose a talk; vote on talks; set up a trade show booth; prepare a talk if chosen to deliver one.
- **What to do during the event to make the most of it**: Visit trade show booths; suggest and schedule meetings betwee humans.
- **What to do after the event**: Share a summary with your team; give Startupfest feedback; sign up for the following year.

Generally this would be encoded in a Skill, hosted on Github by Startupfest. For those who don't yet have an agentic co-founder, we'd offer instructions for how to get started (using Claude, Gemini, or ChatGPT.)

## Open Data Fair

Governments have plenty of data they'd like to share with the world, and are in some cases required to share. Startups can benefit from this data. But it's poorly catalogued, unstructured, and seldom well promoted. We could run a "data open house" in which government departments with data to share host meeting areas where startups can learn about those data sources.

There are other sources (openstreetmaps, commoncrawl, wikimedia, etc.) that are available for use in applications and not commercial offerings, which might be useful to feature as well if we can convince people from those organizations to participate.

## Public/private hackathon

If a government has problems it wants solved, it could issue a request for solutions alongside its shared data. It could even create a structured Markdown document to bring agents up to speed instantly.
